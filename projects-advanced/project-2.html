Interactive Typography

Three interactive poster sketches where motion and interaction support the meaning of each word. Words: Clean, Fragile, Drift.

Clean

A foggy window scene where a squeegee swipe clears the glass and reveals clean.

Fragile

An egg drops and cracks, and the yolk spreads into a puddle that spells fragile.

Drift

Blow on a dandelion and the seeds drift across the canvas, gradually forming drift.

Reflection

Started with the three words and treated each one like an interaction prompt, not just a text prompt. Before making anything polished, prototyped the core gesture for each sketch on a plain background to check one thing. The interaction needed to communicate the word in a few seconds without instructions.

For Clean, the first tests were just rectangles erasing a fog layer. That version worked conceptually but looked too digital, so it got rebuilt as a window scene with a squeegee cursor, a heavier frame, and a wipe that leaves more believable streak spacing. Most of the refinement was tuning the stamp spacing so fast swipes do not look dashed, and making the word readable only after enough clearing happens.

For Fragile, the earliest prototype had the egg dropping immediately, but it felt too sudden and there was no reason to interact. The click-to-rock phase came first, then a clearer state change into dropping, then the split into two shell halves so the impact feels earned. The yolk word started as particles snapping to letter points and it looked too rigid, so the morph got slowed down and drag plus jitter got added. The particle spill also got staggered so it spreads like liquid before resolving into the word. The slight blur in the egg sketch is intentional. It is meant to feel like soft focus and adds to the messiness around the egg and yolk.

For Drift, the first version moved points straight from the flower to the word and it felt mechanical. The final version uses a hover zone to activate the dandelion and a touch radius to catch points gradually, so it reads more like drifting seeds than a teleport. The drift-off and reset phase came last, after the word formation felt stable, so the loop reads like an ongoing breeze instead of a one-time animation.

Overall, the process was prototype interaction first, then build the visual world around it, then tune timing, thresholds, and motion until the behavior feels like the word instead of an effect sitting on top of text.
