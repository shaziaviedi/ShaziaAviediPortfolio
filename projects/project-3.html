Experimental Clock — Metamorph

A two-minute clock told through six chrysalises and their butterflies. Every 20 seconds, a new butterfly hatches, circles its orbit, and leaves a highlighted trail of where time just passed.

Overview

The clock measures 120 seconds as a small ecosystem instead of a number. Six chrysalises hang along a curved branch. Every 20 seconds, another chrysalis moves from default to cracked to open, and a butterfly takes flight. Each butterfly orbits the center once in 20 seconds on its own dashed ring, which briefly glows where it just flew. When the last orbit finishes, all six butterflies drift away and fade.

How it works

Time is driven by millis() and split into 20-second intervals. Arrays store the chrysalis positions, ring radii, and per-butterfly timing. Each ring is precomputed as a set of dash segments, and the sketch draws a faint base ring plus a darker highlight behind the current butterfly, mapped from seconds to angle around the circle.

The chrysalises sit on a cosine-based arc that matches the branch, with small per-cocoon offsets so they feel nestled into the illustration. Butterflies scale down on inner rings to suggest depth, and after the main 140-second sequence, each one is handed off to a dispersal state: a simple physics update that gives them a launch direction, friction, spin, and a timed fade-out. A small numeric clock in the corner grounds the piece in familiar timekeeping.

Reflection

Watching this clock made two minutes feel different. Before the visuals and phases were in place, 120 seconds felt slow and empty. Once the chrysalises started cracking, hatching, looping, and leaving trails, the same span felt shorter and more structured. My attention hopped between “who is about to hatch” and “which ring is currently glowing,” instead of staring at a changing number.

The project taught me how pacing and expectation reshape our sense of time. Clear states, readable motion, and small “events” turn waiting into anticipation. Technically, it was a chance to practice structuring time with arrays and state, mapping seconds to angles, drawing reusable dashed arcs, and decoupling the stationary rings from the butterflies so the trail reads as a memory of movement rather than just motion blur.
