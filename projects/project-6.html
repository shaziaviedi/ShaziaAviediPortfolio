Data Portrait — Water Intake Waves

A weekly water-intake diary translated into stacked 3D waves. Each horizontal sheet represents one glass of water, and hovering over a day reveals its wave stack in motion.

Overview

This data portrait visualizes my daily water intake throughout the week. The personal habit I was tracking was how many glasses of water I drink each day. Each horizontal wave represents one glass of water, and the number of stacked waves corresponds to the total glasses I drank on that day. The color gradient from deep teal to lighter cyan represents time and variation between days, creating a calm but dynamic visualization that feels like moving water.

How to read it

Each day of the week is displayed in the footer bar. Hovering over a day reveals its corresponding wave pattern in 3D, where each horizontal sheet equals one glass of water. Taller stacks indicate days when I drank more water. The viewer can orbit around the waves to explore their shapes from different angles, and double-clicking a day locks the display in place for closer viewing. A single click unlocks the portrait and returns it to hover mode.

Process & reflection

I started by recording how many glasses of water I drank each day for a week and then used Perlin noise to generate flowing 3D surfaces that rise and fall gently. I experimented with different color schemes, eventually choosing darker aquamarine tones to keep the atmosphere calm but rich. Interaction became an important part of the design: the hover and lock mechanics let the viewer move between comparing days and studying a single day in more detail.

Through tracking my water intake, I noticed that my hydration habits are closely linked to how busy or relaxed my schedule is. On days when I had more free time, I naturally remembered to drink more water, while on busier days I often forgot until late at night. Some of this context disappears in the visualization—the waves communicate rhythm and volume, but not mood or stress. Still, turning the numbers into motion made those invisible routines feel more tangible, and helped me see how small habits are shaped by my daily environment and energy.
