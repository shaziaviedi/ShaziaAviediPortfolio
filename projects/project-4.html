Exquisite Corpse — The Party Never Ends

A radioactive platypus character, equal parts cute and unsettling. Built as a hand-coded portrait in p5.js, with a tiny party hat that turns pollution into permanent costume.

Design Process

For this project, I designed a non-human creature based on a platypus. I wanted a character that felt both humorous and unsettling, so I pushed surreal proportions and unexpected details: a muscular belly, clawed limbs, and a tiny party hat that looks almost too cheerful for the body it sits on.

The starting point was a commentary on environmental decay. After a law is passed allowing corporations to dump toxic waste and trash into rivers, the platypus—once a harmless, shy animal—mutates into a radioactive being. Its skin grows thick and discolored, its form becomes distorted, and the only remnants of its old life are pieces of trash that cling to it, like the party hat it now permanently wears. The hat, ironically festive, becomes a symbol of human negligence: a celebration of pollution.

I first drew the figure traditionally using pen on paper, then scanned and refined the design digitally. The exaggerated anatomy and comically small accessories emphasize the absurdity of mutation caused by human irresponsibility. In p5.js, the creature is built from modular functions (head, bill, torso, limbs, tail, hat), which will later make it easier to recombine or animate different body sections for the “exquisite corpse” portion of the assignment.

Background Story · “The Party Never Ends”

Deep in a river once teeming with life, the platypus now rules alone. Decades of waste have turned the water neon green and thick as syrup. This radioactive platypus—nicknamed “Party Pete” in local legends—glows faintly under the moonlight. His hat, a relic of discarded celebration, has fused to his head as his body mutates beyond nature’s design.

Each night, he emerges from the riverbanks, humming the faint echo of party songs distorted by time and water. He wanders through empty marshes, the hat’s bells jingling softly, as if mocking the humans who once partied while the world burned. To the rare person who sees him, Pete is both tragic and terrifying—a lonely survivor of human joy turned into eternal irony.

Reflection

Working on this assignment alone shifted my focus toward narrative consistency rather than collaboration. Instead of reacting to other people’s drawings, I treated the “exquisite corpse” premise as an internal mashup: combining cute and grotesque, natural and artificial, innocent and toxic, all inside one body.

The process showed me how form and story feed each other. As I pushed the anatomy into something more absurd, the environmental backstory naturally darkened to match. Translating the drawing into code made me think about modularity and abstraction—how to break down organic shapes into reusable, named functions that can be rearranged later.

Even though the character is intentionally ridiculous, this radioactive platypus ended up as a small reflection on pollution, mutation, and survival. It reminded me that humor and tragedy can live in the same design, and that code can give even bizarre, accidental creatures a second digital life.
