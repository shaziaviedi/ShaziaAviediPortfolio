Autobiographical Game — Indomie Run

A short autobiographical horror game about late-night cravings, homesickness, and an Indonesian ghost haunting a small NYC bedroom. Collect ten packets of Indomie before the pocong catches you.

Overview

Indomie Run is an autobiographical mini-game about midnight cravings, homesickness, and fear. You play as me in a cramped NYC bedroom, trying to collect ten packets of Indomie—my comfort food from home—while an Indonesian ghost (a pocong) starts to appear and chase you. Pulling in elements like instant noodles as emotional comfort and the pocong from Indonesian folklore turns what could have been a generic chase game into something that feels specific to my own experiences.

How to play

Use the arrow keys to move around the room. Hold Space to sprint, and press E to grab packets when they are placed on furniture. A small HUD keeps track of how many Indomie packets you’ve collected and shows your stamina bar; sprinting drains it and makes you slower when you’re exhausted.

The game unfolds in three scenes: a warm-toned intro where you explore the bedroom, a blue-tinted chase once the ghost fully appears, and an escape cut-scene if you manage to collect all ten packets and reach the door in time. If the ghost catches you, a jumpscare and lose screen appear with a replay option.

How it works

Under the hood, the game is built around classes for the Player, Ghost, Collectible, and Obstacle, plus a simple scene system for the intro, chase, escape cut-scene, and lose overlay. Frame counts drive all the timing: when intro lines appear, when the “something doesn’t feel right” and “don’t look back” overlays fade in and out, when the ghost is revealed, and how long the escape sequence lasts.

Packets spawn randomly each run, some on the floor and some on furniture that require pressing E. The ghost uses basic steering toward the player, with bobbing motion and directional sprites to feel more alive. Warm vs. cool tints, vignette overlays, and UI text all help stage the mood shift from cozy to unsettling while keeping the space readable.

Reflection

Working on Indomie Run pushed me to treat code as a way to choreograph emotion and pacing, not just collisions and movement. I had to think about how scenes, timers, overlays, and camera framing all work together to create tension, instead of only focusing on whether the ghost AI and stamina system “work.”

Incorporating fragments of my culture—like the pocong and Indomie as late-night comfort food—made the game feel grounded in my own life rather than just another horror trope. If I continue the project, I’d like to add sound design and refine the art and animation, but even in this version it feels like a small, complete expression of missing home, being scared, and still chasing comfort at midnight.
